# Project Summary

## Overview

This project presents a reproducible data analytics workflow investigating the factors influencing sustainable practice adoption among built environment professionals in Nigeria. Developed as part of an independent research study, the project combines statistical analysis, data visualization, and scientific interpretation to examine why sustainable practices remain unevenly implemented despite growing awareness of sustainability principles.

Using Python and SPSS, the project reproduces and validates all statistical analyses supporting the accompanying manuscript while demonstrating a transparent and reproducible analytical workflow suitable for research, policy analysis, and professional practice.

Beyond identifying statistical relationships, the project introduces the **Adoption–Contribution Gap**, a conceptual framework that distinguishes between sustainability domains professionals currently implement and those where they believe they can contribute most. Rather than treating implementation gaps simply as evidence of low awareness, the framework identifies where structural interventions are most likely to unlock unrealized professional capacity.

---

# Background

Nigeria's rapid urbanisation presents both significant opportunities and substantial sustainability challenges. Increasing demand for housing, transportation, water infrastructure, waste management, and climate adaptation has placed growing pressure on cities and the professionals responsible for planning, designing, constructing, and managing them.

Although national sustainability policies and international commitments such as the Paris Agreement have increased awareness of sustainable urban development, implementation remains inconsistent across the built environment sector. Previous research has frequently examined policy development, green building technologies, or organisational sustainability initiatives. Comparatively less attention has been given to the practical conditions that enable professionals to translate sustainability knowledge into day-to-day practice.

This project addresses that gap by examining sustainable practice adoption from the perspective of built environment professionals themselves.

---

# Research Objectives

The study was designed to achieve the following objectives:

- Examine the current adoption of sustainable practices among built environment professionals.
- Assess awareness of sustainability and climate policies.
- Identify the factors influencing sustainable practice adoption.
- Evaluate the influence of infrastructure availability on implementation.
- Compare current professional practices with perceived areas of greatest contribution.
- Develop evidence-based recommendations for sustainable urban development and urban governance.

---

# Dataset

The analysis is based on survey responses collected from **147 built environment professionals** representing multiple disciplines across Nigeria.

Participants included professionals working in areas such as:

- Architecture
- Urban Planning
- Civil and Structural Engineering
- Estate Management
- Quantity Surveying
- Environmental Management
- Building Construction
- Related built environment professions

The dataset contains demographic information, professional characteristics, sustainability awareness measures, infrastructure indicators, organisational practices, and responses relating to sustainable practice adoption.

Personally identifiable information has been excluded from the publicly shared analytical dataset.

# Analytical Workflow

The project follows a structured analytical workflow designed to ensure transparency and reproducibility.

The workflow includes:

1. Data cleaning and preprocessing.
2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).
3. Descriptive statistical analysis.
4. Correlation analysis.
5. Multiple linear regression modelling.
6. Model diagnostics.
7. Publication-quality visualization.
8. Scientific interpretation.
9. Independent validation of Python outputs against SPSS analyses.

Python served as the primary analytical environment for reproducibility and visualization, while SPSS was used to independently verify statistical outputs reported in the accompanying manuscript.

# Key Findings

Several important findings emerged from the analysis.

## Infrastructure drives implementation

Infrastructure availability consistently emerged as the strongest predictor of sustainable practice adoption across multiple regression models. This finding suggests that implementation depends less on professional motivation alone and more on whether practitioners operate within environments that enable sustainable decision-making.

---

## Awareness alone is insufficient

Although respondents demonstrated relatively high levels of sustainability awareness, awareness variables explained comparatively little variation in sustainable practice adoption once infrastructure and organisational factors were considered.

This suggests that increasing awareness alone is unlikely to produce substantial improvements without corresponding investments in implementation capacity.

---

## Adoption differs across sustainability domains

Current implementation was highest for sustainability practices that are comparatively accessible within existing organisational and project structures, including:

- Waste Management
- Energy Efficiency

Conversely, implementation remained comparatively low in:

- Green Infrastructure
- Climate-responsive Design
- Water Conservation

These domains were nevertheless identified by respondents as those where professionals could make the greatest long-term contribution.

---

## The Adoption–Contribution Gap

One of the principal contributions of this project is the development of the **Adoption–Contribution Gap**.

This framework compares:

- Current implementation of sustainable practices.
- Professionals' perceived areas of greatest contribution.

The resulting gap provides an evidence-based approach for identifying sustainability domains where targeted investments, institutional reforms, and policy interventions are likely to produce the greatest implementation gains.

Rather than concluding that professionals require greater awareness, the findings indicate that professionals already recognise where they can contribute most. The greater challenge lies in creating the institutional, infrastructural, financial, and governance conditions necessary for implementation.

---

# Practical Implications

The findings have implications for researchers, policymakers, and built environment practitioners.

For policymakers, the results suggest that sustainability programmes should prioritise enabling implementation through improved infrastructure, governance, financing mechanisms, and institutional support rather than relying exclusively on awareness campaigns.

For professional organisations, the findings identify Green Infrastructure, Climate-responsive Design, and Water Conservation as strategic priorities for future capacity development.

For researchers, the project demonstrates how combining quantitative and qualitative evidence can produce richer explanations of implementation challenges than either approach alone.

# Conclusion

This project demonstrates how reproducible data analytics can support evidence-based urban sustainability research. By integrating statistical modelling, visualization, and scientific interpretation, the study moves beyond describing implementation challenges to identifying where interventions are likely to have the greatest impact.

The central contribution of the project is the **Adoption–Contribution Gap**, which provides a practical framework for understanding the difference between what built environment professionals currently implement and where they believe they can contribute most. The findings suggest that future progress depends less on increasing awareness and more on strengthening the infrastructure, governance, institutional capacity, and enabling conditions that allow professionals to translate sustainability knowledge into practice.
